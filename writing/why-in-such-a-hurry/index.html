Why in Such a Hurry?

My daughter asked me why I walk so fast when we go for a stroll.

I told her that I got used to it. Shaped by the pace of the cities, where I became a body in motion, moving from point A to point B from Monday to Friday on repeat, looping.

I told myself that when I am walking, I am thinking about where I am going. I am not present. I am not just walking, I am walking somewhere. This “somewhere” is not here.

I have been walking “somewhere” instead of walking “here”.

First school on schedule, then work on schedule... while walking “somewhere”.

This “somewhere”, where is it? I have been “somewhere” for the last 41 years. I want to be “here”, but I see everyone walking “somewhere”, so I continue the walk.

Why do I think about Monday on Sunday, and about Friday on Thursday?

Always “somewhere”, not “here”.

I have fallen into a pattern and mistaken it for my identity.

My pattern is a result of reacting, same stimulus, same route, same Monday.

How do I transition from reacting to a stimulus, to responding to it?

Stimulus → Reaction.

Stimulus → Breath → Response.

Breath creates the pause. The pause creates the space. The space between stimulus and response is where I experience autonomy, growth, and freedom.

Harold Kushner, in the foreword to Viktor Frankl’s Man’s Search for Meaning, writes:

Life is not primarily a quest for pleasure, as Freud believed, or a quest for power, as Alfred Adler taught, but a quest for meaning. The greatest task for any person is to find meaning in his or her life.

Do I find meaning or create it? Perhaps neither, perhaps I simply start noticing what was always present.

I was looking for “here” while standing in it.

I realised that the object of my search has been right here all along, not “somewhere”.

My daughter asked me why I walk so fast. Maybe she has been here the whole time.
